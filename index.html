Where Robotics Is Heading: Product or Service?

Exploring the fundamental shift in robotics business models, from one-time sales to subscription services, and how this affects the future of the industry.

8 min read â€¢ Business Strategy

As I've been following robotics news and working on my own projects, I noticed something interesting. There seems to be this shift happening in how robotics companies operate. It's not just about building cool robots anymore, it's about how these robots are being used as actual business solutions.

What caught my attention was this question: Should robots be something you buy, like a vacuum cleaner, or something you subscribe to, like Netflix? The more I looked into it, the more I realized this choice actually changes everything about how these companies work.

What The Data Shows

I started digging into some market research, and found some pretty clear trends. Check this out: the service based approach to robotics is growing way faster than traditional consumer robots:

These numbers kind of blew my mind. The service robotics market is expected to hit $213 billion by 2029, huge! Meanwhile, consumer robotics (think robot vacuums)is growing too, but not nearly as fast. As someone interested in both robotics and business, this got me thinking about where the industry is heading.

Cool Companies Doing This

One company that really caught my eye is Zipline. Instead of selling drones, they run an entire delivery service using their drones. They're mainly delivering medical supplies in places where it's hard to reach by road. What's cool is that their customers don't have to worry about maintaining drones or training pilots, Zipline handles everything. It's like a Uber Eats, but for medical supplies (way more important).

This reminds me of how Netflix changed movies. Everything is now a subscription based service. It seems like robotics might be heading in a similar direction.

Following Investment Trends

I also looked into where investors are putting their money (since that's usually a good sign of what's working). The data was pretty interesting:

Even though both types of robotics companies saw a big boost in 2021, service robotics companies have been getting more consistent funding. From what I understand, investors seem to like the idea of steady, recurring revenue instead of one-time sales.

Another Cool Example: Agility Robotics

Another company I thought was cool is Agility Robotics. They've built this impressive humanoid robot called Digit that can work in warehouses. But instead of just selling these robots, they're working closely with companies to integrate them into their operations. It's like having a robotics partner rather than just buying a product.

This makes a lot of sense to me. If I were the one running a warehouse, having help figuring out how to actually use a humanoid robot effectively would be super beneficial.

Why I Think This Matters

As someone learning about robotics and hoping to work in this field, I think this shift is super important. It's changing how we need to think about building robots. It's not just about making a cool robot, it's about creating a whole service that solves real problems for people.

This actually gets me excited about the future. Whether I end up building robots or working on the business side of robotics, understanding this trend feels like getting a peek at where the industry is heading. It's also pretty cool to see how robotics is becoming more about ongoing relationships with customers rather than just selling products.

Why I Started Building Outside of Class

A personal reflection on the journey from textbook problems to real-world projects, and why hands-on building became essential to my learning.

6 min read â€¢ Personal Growth

I hit a point in school where I realized just doing the assignments wasn't enough. Not because the work was easy, but because it didn't feel like it added up to anything real. I could solve textbook problems, follow lab instructions, and pass exams. But I couldn't point to something and say, "I built this."

That started bothering me.

Funnily enough, the first time I set out to do something about it was because of a real-life problem. My roommates never cleaned their dishes, and worse than that, no one took responsibility for the mess. I deided it could be funny and cool to finally put an end to this issue. So I decided to try something different. I grabbed a camera, started playing around with motion detection, and tried to build a system that could figure out who left dishes in the kitchen. No guide, no instructions, just a problem I was tired of dealing with. Once completed, I felt a sense of accomplishment and pride.

That was the first time I saw what it felt like to use what I was learning to solve something that mattered to me.

Why I Needed More Than Coursework

Courses teach important stuff. But the structure of school means you rarely go past what you're told to do. You're learning in pieces, and there's not much time to step back and try putting those pieces together into something that actually works in the real world.

I wanted to build something that forced me to connect the dots with concepts I learned. Something that would break if I misunderstood a voltage rating, or missed a step in the code. Projects do that. They test whether you really get it, or if you just know how to pass the quiz.

Why I Actually Enjoy It

It turns out, it's fun to be stuck. When you're troubleshooting a bug or trying to figure out why your servo isn't moving the way you expected, you're learning. There's frustration, but there's also satisfaction when you solve it. That kind of trial and error sticks with you way more than a lecture ever could.

I also realized that building stuff just makes school more interesting. Suddenly a class on electronics doesn't feel abstract when you're brainstorming how your going to power your own robotic hand. You care more because you're using the material to make something work.

It Builds Real Skills

More than anything, doing your own projects makes you more capable. You get used to failing without panicking. You learn how to take a vague idea and make it real.

There's something different about finishing something you made from scratch. It gives you confidence. It makes you curious to try something harder next time. And it makes you feel like the material in school can be more than a chore.

My Journey

Kitchen Vision Alert System

My journey into computer vision began with a common roommate challenge - dirty dishes. I developed an alert system that could detect when dishes were left on the counter, and by who, sending friendly reminders to keep our space clean. This project sparked my fascination with computer vision's potential to solve everyday problems, setting me on a path that would grow far beyond simple detection systems.

Tech Stack

Captured live video feed from QuickTime Player and applied real-time contour-based object tracking using OpenCV at around 30 FPS. Although this approach worked initially for detecting dish placements, pure contour tracking proved inefficient under complex backgrounds and varied lighting conditions.

  • Python
  • OpenCV
  • QuickTime Player

Gesture-Controlled Smart Lighting

Ready to bridge the gap between software and hardware, I created a system that controlled my room's LED lights through simple hand gestures. With just a pinch of my fingers, I could turn lights on/off and cycle through colors. This project marked my first step into the physical world, combining computer vision with real-world hardware control.

Tech Stack

Utilized MediaPipe's 21-point hand tracking at ~30 FPS to capture gesture coordinates. Serialized 3D gesture vectors over UART at 115200 baud, achieving sub-30 ms end-to-end latency. LED updates run at 60 Hz, enabling smooth brightness and color transitions.

  • Python
  • MediaPipe
  • OpenCV

Robotic Hand

My most ambitious project yet combined everything I'd learned: a 3D-printed robotic hand that mirrors human hand movements in real-time. This project merged computer vision, hardware control, and some mechanical learning into one cohesive system. Watching the robotic hand move in perfect sync with my own was a defining moment that confirmed my passion for creating robots that can interact naturally with humans.

Tech Stack

Assembled from open-source STL models providing 5 degrees of freedom. MediaPipe hand tracking supplies 21 3D landmarks at ~30 FPS; finger open/closed states are determined by comparing tip-to-base landmark distances. Servo commands run at 100 Hz, achieving sub-15 ms actuation latency.

  • Python
  • MediaPipe
  • OpenCV
  • Fusion 360

CaseAI

After my robotic hand, I wanted to explore how AI could transform learning. I built CaseAI to solve a real problem I'd experienced myself: struggling to practice case interviews without access to experienced interviewers. The system conducts natural conversations, listens to responses through speech recognition, and provides detailed feedback on performance. Watching candidates improve their skills through AI-guided practice sessions showed me the potential of AI tools at a business / educational level.

Tech Stack

Built with GPT-4 for intelligent conversation management and Whisper for real-time speech-to-text processing. Features a progressive grading framework that evaluates performance across multiple interview stages, providing candidates with comprehensive feedback and scoring metrics. The system enables natural conversation flow while maintaining structured evaluation criteria.

  • React
  • FastAPI
  • NLP
  • Conversational AI

AI Powered Voice Receptionist

My first automation project where I created a functioning AI voice receptionist with Vapi and n8n. This project marked my entry into the world of business automation, combining voice AI technology with workflow automation to create a seamless customer experience. Building this system taught me how AI can streamline business operations and improve customer service efficiency.

Tech Stack

Built with Vapi for AI voice processing and n8n for workflow automation. The system handles incoming calls, processes customer requests, and automates responses and routing for efficient customer service operations.

  • Vapi
  • n8n
  • Voice AI
  • Automation

Investment Memo Automation

During my internship, I built an automated system that scoured the internet with Perplexity Deep Research for any relevant information regarding the company at hand and used RAG to gather insights from internal documents. I created a system that produced consistent, high-quality reports in a standardized format that the investment team could immediately use for decision-making. This project showed me that very complex automations can produce professional-grade outputs that rival human work while maintaining consistency and efficiency.

Tech Stack

Built with Perplexity Deep Research for comprehensive data gathering and RAG to process internal documents. The system generates standardized investment memos with consistent formatting and quality, significantly reducing research time for the investment team.

  • Web Scraping
  • RAG
  • Python
  • Document Processing
